# Revenue Creation Cohorts

> Part of the Revenue Analytics Framework. See [revenue-analytics.md](../revenue-analytics.md) for the index.

---

## Purpose

Revenue creation cohorts measure the performance of new revenue — revenue from customers who were not paying in the previous period. This is distinct from repeat revenue measurement (see `analytics/03-repeat-revenue-states.md`), which tracks existing customers.

**Separating these two pools is critical.** A business with strong new customer acquisition but poor retention will look healthy in aggregate until the leaky bucket effect compounds. Cohort analysis makes the leak visible.

---

## What a Revenue Creation Cohort Is

A revenue creation cohort is a group of customers who made their **first payment** in a defined period (typically a month).

```
January cohort = all customers who paid for the first time in January
February cohort = all customers who paid for the first time in February
...
```

Each cohort is tracked from its starting point forward, measuring:
- How much revenue did this cohort generate in month 0 (their first month)?
- How much revenue did the same cohort generate in month 1, 3, 6, 12?
- At what rate is each cohort expanding, stable, or declining over time?

## Building a Cohort Revenue Table

```
Cohort   | M0      | M1      | M3      | M6      | M12
---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|--------
Jan      | $1,000  | $870    | $720    | $610    | $450
Feb      | $1,200  | $1,020  | $840    | $710    | —
Mar      | $1,100  | $990    | $800    | —       | —
Apr      | $1,400  | $1,260  | —       | —       | —
```

**Reading the table:**
- **January cohort at M12:** $450 of the original $1,000 remains → 45% retained; 55% churned
- **Month 1 retention improving:** Jan 87% → Feb 85% → Mar 90% → activation/onboarding may be improving
- **Month 3 drop-off consistent at ~70–73%:** Suggests a common 90-day churn trigger (habit not forming, or a specific friction point)

## Cohort Velocity — Is the Business Accelerating?

Cohort velocity compares the revenue generated by successive cohorts at the same lifecycle stage.

```
Velocity = Revenue of cohort N at period P ÷ Revenue of cohort N−1 at period P

Example (comparing cohorts at M0):
  Jan cohort M0: $1,000
  Feb cohort M0: $1,200
  Velocity = 1.20 → cohorts are growing 20% month-over-month at acquisition
```

**Velocity interpretation:**

| Velocity | Signal | Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 1.2 | Strong acquisition growth | Investigate which channel is driving growth; protect and scale it |
| 1.0–1.2 | Stable acquisition | Monitor; ensure channel mix is healthy |
| 0.8–1.0 | Slowing acquisition | Diagnose: channel saturation, pricing barrier, or product-market fit drift |
| < 0.8 | Declining acquisition | Urgent: acquisition model has a structural problem |

Track velocity at multiple lifecycle stages (M0, M3, M6) — not just at M0 — to separate acquisition volume from activation quality.

## Cohort Revenue Retention Curve

Plot each cohort's revenue retention on a single chart with time (months) on the x-axis and % of M0 revenue retained on the y-axis. Each cohort becomes a line on the chart.

**What to look for:**

**Pattern 1 — Smile curve (revenue grows after initial drop):**
```
M0: 100% → M1: 80% → M3: 75% → M6: 85% → M12: 90%
```
Expansion is outpacing churn from month 6 onward. Healthy expansion-led retention. Usually indicates strong upgrade triggers or usage-based growth.

**Pattern 2 — Gradual decline to stable floor:**
```
M0: 100% → M1: 85% → M3: 70% → M6: 65% → M12: 63%
```
Churn happens early then stabilises. Users who survive past 3 months are likely to stay. Focus activation efforts on the M0→M3 transition.

**Pattern 3 — Continued decline with no floor:**
```
M0: 100% → M1: 80% → M3: 60% → M6: 40% → M12: 20%
```
Ongoing churn at every stage. No stable subscriber base forming. Product-market fit or value proposition problem.

## Identifying the Churn Moment

Cohort analysis localises *when* churn happens, which points to *why*:

| When Churn Spikes | Likely Cause | Diagnostic |
|---|---|---|
| M0 → M1 (first month) | User never activated; failed to reach Aha Moment | Check onboarding completion rates; time to first value |
| M1 → M3 | Habit didn't form; product used sporadically then abandoned | Check DAU/WAU ratio; feature engagement depth |
| M3 → M6 | Feature exhaustion or competitive switch | Check feature usage breadth; run exit surveys |
| M11 → M12 (annual renewal) | User doesn't perceive enough value to renew at full price | Check engagement leading up to renewal; proactive outreach |

## Comparing Cohort Quality Across Acquisition Channels

Not all cohorts are created equal. Tag cohorts by acquisition channel to identify which channels produce higher-quality customers.

```
Cohort segment analysis (M6 retention by channel):

Channel           | M0 Revenue | M6 Retention | M6 Revenue
Organic search    | $800       | 68%          | $544
Paid social       | $1,200     | 48%          | $576
Referral          | $600       | 75%          | $450
App store         | $900       | 61%          | $549
```

In this example:
- Paid social generates the most M0 revenue but has the lowest retention — likely attracting price-sensitive users
- Referral generates the least M0 revenue but has the highest retention — referral users have stronger intent
- Organic search is the most balanced channel

**Implication:** Optimising purely for M0 revenue per channel overweights paid social. Optimising for M6 revenue per channel shifts investment toward organic and referral.

## Minimum Viable Cohort Tracking Setup

For teams early in building analytics:

1. **Tag every subscriber at first payment** with: cohort month, acquisition channel, plan, billing cycle, market/geography
2. **Export a monthly snapshot** of which subscribers are still active (and their current MRR contribution)
3. **Join on cohort tags** to build the cohort table in any BI tool or spreadsheet

Even a manual monthly export with basic spreadsheet cohort analysis is significantly more informative than aggregate MRR tracking alone.

## Common Mistakes in Cohort Analysis

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| Using aggregate churn rate instead of cohort retention | Aggregate rate mixes old and new cohorts; can be stable or declining while individual cohorts worsen |
| Starting cohort at signup (not first payment) | Non-paying users are not revenue cohorts; include them only in funnel analysis, not revenue cohort analysis |
| Not separating cohorts by acquisition channel | Blended cohorts conceal that one channel is dragging down aggregate retention |
| Drawing conclusions from M1 data only | M1 dropout may look catastrophic; the real story is whether the remaining cohort stabilises |
| Not tracking expansion within cohorts | A cohort that declines in subscriber count but grows in revenue is expanding from its retained base — a healthy pattern |
